Overview - Tirohanga whānui

New Zealand faces increasing environmental and economic pressures as the electric vehicles (EVs) gaining popularity, showing the need for sustainable battery recycling infrastructure. Without effective recycling processes, the disposal of EV batteries poses environmental risks, including potential soil and water contamination from toxic elements during battery recycling process.


This website presents study on how Aotearoa can build a safe, efficient, and economically viable ecosystem for managing electric vehicle (EV) batteries once they reach their end of life.

Key Benefits:


  • Reduced environmental pollution and landfill pressure
  • Material recovery for local manufacturing and renewable storage
  • Economic opportunities for regional recycling industries
  • Second‑life batteries for solar and grid‑support systems

Why it matters? - He Aha te Take Nui

Supports New Zealand's transition toward a circular & low-carbon future.

Environmental Impact

When improperly stored or disposed of, lithium-ion batteries can leach toxic materials such as heavy metals and electrolytes into soil and water systems, causing long-term contamination.
Economic Opportunity

The framework encourages the creation of green jobs in dismantling, materials recovery, and second-life energy applications.
Safety and Risk Management

Clear guidance on safe handling, packaging, and logistics ensures public safety while reducing the likelihood of incidents throughout the recycling lifecycle.

Innovation and technology growth

Accelerates local research, development, and clean-tech innovation in second-life battery storage.

Interesting facts:

End-of-life batteries are first collected and assessed for reuse. Batteries still holding about 70–80 % of their capacity can be repurposed for stationary energy storage.

Yes, lithium-ion batteries are classified as hazardous goods because of their fire and explosion risks. They must be stored and transported according to international safety standards and New Zealand's hazardous substances regulations, with trained personnel managing every step.

At present, most end-of-life EV batteries are stored by importers or exported to overseas facilities for material recovery.

Vehicle owners can ensure their old batteries are returned to authorised collection points, while councils can collaborate with recyclers to establish regional drop-off and storage hubs.
