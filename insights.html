From Growth to Circular Opportunity

New Zealand’s electric vehicle (EV) market is accelerating at an unprecedented rate. In 2020, fewer than 20,000 EVs were registered nationwide. By 2035, that number is expected to exceed 400,000 vehicles, representing one of the fastest adoption curves in the Southern Hemisphere.

This dramatic rise will also bring a new environmental challenge, a growing pipeline of end-of-life batteries. Within the next decade, tens of thousands of lithium-ion batteries will reach retirement. Instead of becoming waste, these batteries represent a valuable new resource for reuse, repurposing, and recycling.

The Current Landscape

At present, most used EV batteries in New Zealand are exported overseas for processing. The country lacks a fully operational domestic recycling plant, and transporting batteries between the North and South Islands adds to logistical costs. There is also no mandatory product-stewardship scheme to ensure responsible end-of-life management. As a result, valuable materials such as lithium, cobalt, and nickel are lost to international markets, while local innovators miss out on economic opportunities. The system is functional, but not yet sustainable.

Challenges to Overcome

The report identifies several major barriers that New Zealand must address to build a viable EV battery recycling industry.

  • Infrastructure — establishing local testing, repurposing, and recycling facilities
  • Cost - as small market size and long transport distances increase financial pressure.
  • Workforce capability - since few technicians currently have training in high-voltage battery handling, chemical recovery, or safety management.

Finally, there is a need for stronger regulation and traceability to reduce fire and environmental risks during storage and transport.
Without these building blocks, the nation risks continuing to rely on offshore processing and missing out on developing a home-grown circular-economy industry.

Emerging Opportunities

Despite these challenges, the path ahead is full of promise. Second-life energy storage represents one of the most immediate opportunities, using repurposed EV batteries to store solar or wind energy for homes, farms, and communities.
New Zealand’s clean-energy transition creates ideal conditions for this type of local innovation.
Developing recycling and refurbishment facilities will also generate skilled, future-focused jobs in engineering, logistics, and environmental management.
Partnerships between universities, start-ups, and recycling firms can accelerate the use of AI-assisted diagnostics and robotic disassembly to improve safety and efficiency.
On a regional level, collaborating with Australia and Pacific neighbours could help New Zealand scale processing volumes and share infrastructure costs.
These opportunities align strongly with the country’s sustainability agenda and position Aotearoa as a leader in clean-tech innovation.

Policy Alignment and National Vision

The policy landscape is already shifting in the right direction.
The Waste Minimisation Act (2008) and Te Rautaki Pāra – Waste Strategy (2023) both encourage circular use of resources, while the Emissions Reduction Plan supports low-carbon technologies and waste reduction.
Together, these policies create a framework for introducing a national product-stewardship programme for EV batteries, ensuring that manufacturers, importers, and recyclers share responsibility for the full battery lifecycle.
This policy foundation, paired with industry innovation, will enable New Zealand to move from exporting waste to retaining value locally.

A Circular Future

By 2035, New Zealand has the potential to establish a fully circular EV battery economy.
In this vision, every battery is tracked from production to end-of-life, assessed for reuse, repurposed for energy storage, and finally recycled to recover valuable materials for new manufacturing.
Automation and AI ensure that each step is safe, efficient, and transparent.

New Zealand’s EV revolution is more than a transport story, it’s an opportunity to build a self-sustaining, circular battery ecosystem that powers the nation’s clean-energy future.
