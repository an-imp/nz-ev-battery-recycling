Overview

Every electric vehicle (EV) battery has a journey — from its creation to its eventual recycling or repurposing. Understanding the EV battery lifecycle is essential for building a sustainable, circular framework that captures value at every stage while reducing environmental impact.

This lifecycle illustrates how materials are extracted, transformed into batteries, used in vehicles, and then recovered or reused — closing the loop through circular design, reuse, repurposing, and recycling.

Use Phase — In-Vehicle Operation

After installation, the battery powers the electric vehicle throughout its operational life. Typically, an EV battery will serve 8–10 years before its capacity drops below the performance threshold required for transport use.

During this period, battery management systems (BMS) continuously monitor temperature, voltage, and charge cycles to ensure efficiency and safety.

When a battery's usable capacity falls to around 70–80%, it no longer meets vehicle performance needs but still holds considerable energy potential for other applications.

Reuse and Repurposing

Once removed from a vehicle, the battery is assessed to determine whether it can be reused or repurposed. Batteries with good structural integrity may be refurbished and returned to service, while others can be adapted for stationary energy storage systems, such as solar or wind energy backup.

Repurposing extends the useful life of the battery by another 5–10 years, providing cost-effective and sustainable energy solutions for homes, businesses, and communities.

This stage represents one of the most impactful steps toward achieving a circular economy, as it delays the recycling process and maximises the value extracted from each battery.

End-of-Life Collection and Recycling

When batteries reach the end of their usable life, they enter the recycling phase. This process involves safe collection, dismantling, and material recovery using technologies such as pyrometallurgical, hydrometallurgical, and direct recycling methods.

Recovered materials like lithium, cobalt, nickel, and copper can be reintroduced into new battery production, reducing reliance on mining and contributing to resource security.
