Overview

Electric vehicle (EV) battery recycling is a global challenge that has inspired innovation across many regions.

This section demos key international case studies, from the European Union, China, Japan, and the United States, and highlights how their experiences inform Aotearoa's path toward a circular EV battery economy.

European Union – Regulatory Leadership and Producer Responsibility

The European Union (EU) leads the world in policy-driven recycling systems. Under the EU Battery Directive (2006/66/EC) and the upcoming EU Battery Regulation (2023), battery producers are legally responsible for the collection, treatment, and recycling of end-of-life batteries.
EU member states have established nationwide collection networks and stringent recycling-efficiency targets—currently 65 % for lithium-ion batteries.
Companies such as Umicore (Belgium) and ACCUREC (Germany) use advanced hydrometallurgical and pyrometallurgical methods to recover high-value metals like cobalt, nickel, and lithium at industrial scale.

China – Industrial Scale and Technological Innovation

China operates one of the most comprehensive EV battery recycling networks, Manufacturers such as CATL and GEM Co. Ltd work closely with vehicle makers to ensure full lifecycle traceability using a national data-tracking platform.
China’s recyclers employ hydrometallurgical and direct-recycling technologies to recover materials with high efficiency, while pilot projects explore automated disassembly and AI-assisted sorting.
Reused and repurposed batteries are also widely adopted for renewable-energy storage and grid-balancing applications.

Japan – Technological Efficiency and Safety Excellence

Japan’s recycling framework focuses on technological precision and safety management.
Companies like Sumitomo Metal Mining and Honda R&D employ closed-loop recycling systems that return recovered materials directly into new battery production.
Strict government regulations under the Home Appliance Recycling Act and Waste Management Law enforce safe transport, storage, and processing.
Japan has also pioneered battery-to-grid (B2G) applications, using second-life EV batteries to stabilise renewable-energy supply during peak demand.

United States – Public-Private Partnerships and Innovation Hubs

In the United States, the EV recycling sector is driven by federal funding and private-sector innovation.
The U.S. Department of Energy’s ReCell Center coordinates national research into advanced recycling methods, focusing on direct-recycling and low-impact hydrometallurgy.
Companies such as Redwood Materials and Li-Cycle operate large-scale facilities that use automation and closed-loop systems to recover and reuse materials domestically.
The U.S. model emphasises public-private partnerships, where collaboration between government, academia, and industry accelerates technology development and commercial adoption.
