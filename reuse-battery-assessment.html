Overview

Before a battery is dismantled or recycled, it undergoes a comprehensive condition and safety assessment to determine whether it can be reused in its original form or repurposed for a new application.

This step is vital in building a circular EV battery ecosystem, where value is retained for as long as possible before end-of-life processing.

The goal of battery assessment is simple: to keep batteries in circulation for as long as it is safe and practical, minimising environmental impact and making the most of the resources already extracted from the earth.

Battery Assessment Process

The assessment process focuses on understanding the State of Health (SOH) of each battery pack. This includes measuring its remaining capacity, voltage stability, and internal resistance. If a battery still retains around 70 to 80 percent of its original capacity, it is often suitable for reuse.

Technicians and researchers use a combination of diagnostic tools — such as voltage and impedance tests, infrared imaging, and data analysis from the battery’s management system — to evaluate its performance. In some cases, AI-based diagnostic software is used to predict lifespan and categorise batteries for reuse, repurposing, or recycling.

This careful testing ensures that only safe, reliable batteries are reintroduced into circulation, preventing accidents and reducing unnecessary recycling costs.

Reuse Pathways

Once a battery passes all necessary safety and performance checks, it can be reused in several ways. Some may be refurbished and reinstalled into vehicles after replacing faulty modules. Others can find a second life powering smaller electric mobility systems, such as e-bikes or scooters, or serve as certified second-use batteries for residential or commercial energy storage.

By reusing EV batteries, New Zealand not only reduces the need for new production but also supports the growth of the renewable energy sector. Each reused battery represents a meaningful reduction in carbon emissions and energy consumption across its extended life cycle.
