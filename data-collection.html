Overview

The data collection process for this research combined qualitative insights and professional perspectives to understand how New Zealand could build a practical, sustainable system for electric vehicle (EV) battery recycling.

Rather than relying solely on academic theory, the research engaged directly with industry experts, ensuring the proposed framework reflects both the opportunities and challenges faced by real-world practitioners.

Data Collection Approach

A semi-structured interview method was selected as the primary data collection tool. This approach allowed for in-depth exploration of key topics while giving participants the flexibility to share their unique experiences and insights.

Four professionals were interviewed, representing both the public and private sectors within New Zealand’s EV battery and recycling ecosystem. Each participant received the interview questions and consent form in advance, following ethical approval processes under the University of Auckland’s research guidelines.

This mix of structured and open-ended questions enabled participants to elaborate on their experiences, leading to richer, more context-specific insights.

Thematic Analysis

All interviews were transcribed, coded, and grouped under common themes.

Through qualitative analysis, several recurring patterns emerged:

  • Infrastructure Gaps – New Zealand currently lacks dedicated large-scale recycling plants; most end-of-life (EoL) batteries are exported offshore for processing.
  • Policy and Regulation – Stakeholders identified the absence of a formal product stewardship framework as a barrier to sustainable operations.
  • Technology and Skills – There is limited local expertise in hydrometallurgical or direct-recycling methods, and workforce training is urgently needed.
  • Economic Feasibility – The high cost of establishing recycling facilities in a small market highlights the importance of regional collaboration and public-private partnerships.
  • Repurposing Potential – Interviewees strongly supported repurposing batteries for solar and grid-energy storage, citing early success from pilot projects overseas.
  • Data and Safety Management – Participants emphasised the need for better tracking systems, safety protocols, and digital monitoring across the battery lifecycle.



Summary of Participant Feedback

Participants were optimistic about the environmental and economic potential of EV battery recycling but expressed caution about scalability and safety.

They highlighted that success will depend on:

  • Government investment in recycling infrastructure and R&D.
  • Industry standards for safe handling, transport, and testing.
  • Collaboration between manufacturers, recyclers, and local councils.
  • Integration of AI and automation to improve efficiency and reduce human risk.

These perspectives provided direct guidance for refining the final framework proposal, ensuring it reflects the realities of New Zealand’s market size and policy environment.
