Overview

The research underpinning this framework was designed to create a practical, evidence-based, and sustainable model for managing end-of-life (EoL) electric vehicle (EV) batteries in New Zealand.

A mixed qualitative approach was adopted combining literature review, semi-structured interviews, and reflective practice to capture both academic knowledge and real-world industry perspectives.

This methodology ensured that the proposed framework is not only conceptually strong but also aligned with local conditions, policy structures, and technological readiness within Aotearoa.

1. Literature Review

The research began with a comprehensive review of academic and professional literature to establish a strong theoretical foundation and identify knowledge gaps.

Sources included:

  • Peer-reviewed journal articles and scholarly books
  • Government and agency reports (e.g., Ministry for the Environment, Waka Kotahi, EECA)
  • Data from professional recycling associations and international policy papers

The review focused on how EV battery recycling frameworks have been implemented globally, in regions such as the EU, China, and the US and what lessons could be applied to New Zealandâ€™s smaller market.

It also revealed gaps in the local system, including the lack of a regulated stewardship scheme, limited recycling infrastructure, and low public awareness.

2. Data Collection through Semi-Structured Interviews

To complement academic findings with industry insights, semi-structured interviews were conducted with four professionals from both public and private sectors who are directly involved in EV battery management and recycling.
This method was chosen for its flexibility, allowing participants to freely share experiences while ensuring that key research questions were consistently addressed.
Each interviewee received the questions in advance and provided written consent before participation.
The interviews explored themes such as:

  • Current challenges and limitations in New Zealandâ€™s EV recycling system
  • Technological readiness for hydrometallurgical and direct-recycling methods
  • Opportunities for collaboration between government, recyclers, and manufacturers
  • Feasibility of repurposing EV batteries for stationary energy storage
Responses from these interviews were coded and analysed to identify recurring patterns, key enablers, and practical recommendations.

3. Reflective Practice and Framework Development

Following the interviews, a reflective practice phase was used to refine and validate the initial draft framework.

This involved evaluating the draft against feedback from industry participants, ensuring it addressed both theoretical soundness and on-the-ground practicality.

Early versions of the framework were heavily influenced by international models, but reflection and stakeholder feedback highlighted the importance of tailoring solutions to New Zealandâ€™s unique economic scale, regulatory context, and energy ecosystem.

One significant improvement that emerged from this process was the emphasis on repurposing EV batteries for energy-storage applications before full recycling, a more achievable and cost-effective approach for New Zealandâ€™s market size.
